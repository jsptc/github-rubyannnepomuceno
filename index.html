Types of Network

PAN · LAN · MAN · WAN — quick descriptions and practical purposes

At a glance

Networks are categorized by scope, scale, and purpose. Below are four common types — Personal, Local, Metropolitan, and Wide area networks — with simple descriptions and real-world purpose statements.

This page uses an external reference image displayed to the right.

1

Personal Area Network (PAN)

Small-range network for a single user

What it is: A PAN connects devices that belong to one person — for example a phone, laptop, smartwatch, and headphones.

Typical technologies: Bluetooth, USB, Zigbee, personal hotspot Wi‑Fi.

Purpose: Enable seamless short-range connectivity for personal devices (file sync, audio, tethering).

Why it matters

PANs simplify daily workflows by letting your personal devices communicate wirelessly. They are usually limited to a few meters and managed by the device owner.

2

Local Area Network (LAN)

Network within a building or campus

What it is: A LAN links computers, printers and servers inside a home, office, or school.

Typical technologies: Ethernet (wired), Wi‑Fi (wireless).

Purpose: High-speed resource sharing, central storage, local services and printing.

Why it matters

LANs provide fast, low-latency connectivity across short distances and are the backbone of most internal networks in homes and organizations.

3

Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)

Network covering a city or large campus

What it is: A MAN connects multiple LANs across a metropolitan area — e.g., municipal networks, university campuses, or city-wide ISP segments.

Typical technologies: Fiber rings, leased lines, carrier Ethernet.

Purpose: Aggregate local networks for regional services and efficient inter-LAN communication.

Why it matters

MANs bridge the gap between local and wide networks, enabling city-scale connectivity and often supporting high-capacity links for businesses and service providers.

4

Wide Area Network (WAN)

Network spanning countries or the globe

What it is: A WAN connects LANs and MANs across long distances and may span cities, countries, or continents.

Typical technologies: MPLS, SD‑WAN, carrier backbones, undersea fiber links.

Purpose: Support long-distance communication between branch offices, cloud datacenters, and global services (the Internet is the largest WAN).

Why it matters

WANs enable enterprises and users to communicate across geopolitical distances and form the infrastructure that powers global applications and internet access.
